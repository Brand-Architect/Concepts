A new identity for Dark Woods Coffee

The Opportunity

A roastery of this standing deserves a mark
that holds its weight on any surface.

Dark Woods carries one of the most compelling origin stories in British specialty coffee — three founders with world-class credentials, a Victorian wool mill in the Pennine valley, and a track record of Great Taste Awards that most roasters will never approach. The current visual identity reflects none of it.

What the current identity is costing you

Three small trees

Three pictogram trees — illustrative, clip-art in character, no typographic architecture. Generic woodland imagery any countryside café could use.

The mark says nothing about the craft, the people, or the depth behind the name.

The story is buried

The homepage opens directly into a product grid — coffees, prices, buy buttons — before a visitor has any reason to care. The Slaithwaite mill, the founders, the obsession: nowhere above the fold.

You are selling before you have earned the right to.

Copy that doesn't ask to be read

The About page runs to wall-to-wall prose with no visual hierarchy, no pause points, no editorial structure. The longest copy on the site is the least likely to be read.

The strongest proof of credibility goes unseen.

The mill setting is invisible

A Victorian textile mill in a Pennine valley — wool, stone, drystone walls, ancient woodland. That is a world-class visual asset. It does not appear in the hero, the navigation, or the brand voice.

The single most distinctive thing about Dark Woods is hiding in the sub-pages.

Product photography does all the work

Coffee bags in a grid is standard across every UK specialty roaster. There is no atmosphere, no editorial tension, no visual language that sets Dark Woods apart from anyone else at a similar price point.

Price becomes the only differentiator when the aesthetics are the same.

Typography as a missed signal

The type system is functional but anonymous. There is no voice in the letterforms — nothing that signals premium provenance, handcraft, or earns the price point before the first word is read.

Typography either builds trust silently or erodes it.

The proposed mark

A DW monogram built with purpose

The interlocking D and W share a structural vertical — the letters relate architecturally, not incidentally. The curled ascender gives atmosphere without affectation. Below it: a wordmark spaced with editorial restraint. The whole reads as considered, premium, and unmistakably Dark Woods.

The Context

Stone, wool,
steam and
obsessive craft.

Dark Woods is based in Slaithwaite — a mill town sitting in a Colne Valley fold, surrounded by moorland and drystone walls, with a history rooted in wool and engineering. The roastery occupies a Victorian textile mill that processed fleece for over a century before coffee arrived.

That setting is extraordinarily rich. It is not being used.

The founders bring credentials that are rare by any measure: world competition judges, SCA qualification authors, sourcing relationships earned across decades of origin travel, over 100 Great Taste Awards, and B Corp certification.

None of that substance reaches the surface of the current brand. The gap between what Dark Woods actually is and what it currently presents as is the entire opportunity.
