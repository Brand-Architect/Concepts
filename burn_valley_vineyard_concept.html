The Opportunity

A vineyard of genuine distinction —
deserving an identity to match.

Burn Valley Vineyard sits in the flinty chalk of North Creake, four miles from the Norfolk coast — a third-generation farming family who planted 17,000 vines on a conviction that this land could produce something exceptional. It can. It does. The brand around it should be equally exceptional.

01

The Current Mark

Their existing logo is a complex photographic composite — handsome at large scale, but at the size of a bottle label neck, a website header, or a social media avatar, it dissolves. A vineyard of this standing needs a mark that commands at every size.

02

The Digital Presence

The website holds good content but the visual hierarchy doesn't yet reflect the quality in the bottle. Visitors should feel the terroir before they read a word. First impressions are set in under three seconds.

03

The Label Opportunity

Award-winning wines deserve award-winning labels. The vineyard cultivates nine varieties — Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Bacchus, Solaris and more. Each deserves a label that does justice to the flinty chalk soil it grew from.

04

The Missed Signal

South-facing. Chalk base. Glyphosate-free. An area of outstanding natural beauty. These are premium provenance signals that serious wine buyers specifically look for — and they are not yet leading the conversation.

The Story Behind the Label

John Robinson bought the farm in 1989. It was his idea to plant vines. His daughter Laura took that idea and ran — planting 17,000 of them across twelve acres of some of Norfolk's finest free-draining chalk.

Four miles from the North Norfolk coast, an area of outstanding natural beauty. Glyphosate-free from the beginning. Wildlife-rich. Carefully tended.

This is a family who didn't compromise. The identity around their wines shouldn't either.

The proposed mark — the BV monogram — is designed to carry that weight. Quietly authoritative. Unambiguous at any size. As at home on a bottle neck label as it is on a cellar door sign.

Typography

Type that carries quiet authority.

English wine has found its confidence. The type system proposed here reflects that — a classical display face with deep roots in English letterpress tradition, paired with a restrained geometric sans for labelling and navigation.
